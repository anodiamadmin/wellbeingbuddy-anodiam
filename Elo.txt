The algorithm addresses all categories while providing a comprehensive framework for understanding a child's overall wellbeing development. It can be used by parents, educators, or healthcare providers to identify areas of strength and opportunities for growth.
This algorithm measures wellbeing development in children and teens (ages 5-16) across eight key dimensions. Each dimension is scored on a scale of 0-50, for a total possible score of 0-400.
Score Interpretation:
•	0-199: Underdeveloped/Developing (typical of ages 5-7)
•	200-299: Average/Sound Development (typical of ages 8-13)
•	300-400: Well-Developed/Thriving (typical of ages 14-16)

Overall Wellbeing Score Interpretation:
0-199: Underdeveloped/Developing Wellbeing (Typical of ages 5-7)
Children in this range are still developing fundamental wellbeing capacities. This is normal and appropriate for younger children (5-7) but concerning for older children. Scores in this range for older children may indicate developmental delays, traumatic experiences, or significant gaps in support systems.
Key characteristics:
•	Heavy reliance on adults for emotional regulation and decision-making
•	Concrete thinking with limited self-reflection
•	Developing basic understanding of emotions, boundaries, and social rules
•	Normal egocentrism appropriate to early development
Potential risks if persistent in older children:
•	Developmental delays across multiple domains
•	Vulnerability to manipulation or exploitation
•	Difficulty forming healthy relationships
•	Poor self-concept and limited sense of agency
•	Foundation for anxiety, depression, or behavioural problems
Recommended approach:
•	Focus on building foundational skills in lagging dimensions
•	Create highly structured, predictable environments
•	Provide significant adult support while gradually building independence
•	Consider developmental assessment if significantly behind age expectations
•	Implement targeted interventions for specific domains scoring below 20
200-299: Average/Sound Wellbeing (Typical of ages 8-13)
Children in this range demonstrate age-appropriate wellbeing across most domains. This represents typical development for middle childhood and early adolescence, with growing independence, social awareness, and self-regulation. 
Key characteristics:
•	Increasing self-regulation and independence
•	Developing more complex understanding of emotions and relationships
•	Growing awareness of others' perspectives and social dynamics
•	Beginning to form personal values and interests
Potential areas for attention:
•	Uneven development across dimensions (some high, some low)
•	Emerging identity questions and social pressures
•	Increased academic and social demands requiring new coping strategies
•	Growing need for autonomy balanced with continued guidance
Recommended approach:
•	Support dimensions scoring below 30 with targeted strategies
•	Provide opportunities for appropriate independence and decision-making
•	Foster connections with positive peer groups and role models
•	Teach more advanced coping strategies and problem-solving skills
•	Encourage exploration of interests and strengths
300-400: Well-Developed/Thriving Wellbeing (Typical of ages 14-16)
Young people in this range demonstrate advanced wellbeing capacities across most or all dimensions. This represents optimal development for adolescents, with sophisticated emotional understanding, strong interpersonal skills, and emerging life direction.
Key characteristics:
•	Strong self-awareness and identity development
•	Effective emotional regulation and diverse coping strategies
•	Well-developed moral reasoning and personal values
•	Healthy relationships and social navigation skills
•	Emerging sense of purpose and future orientation
Areas for continued growth: 
•	Refining life purpose and long-term goals
•	Navigating increasingly complex relationships
•	Preparing for transitions to adulthood
•	Deepening philosophical understanding and ethical reasoning
Recommended approach:
•	Provide mentorship opportunities in areas of strength and interest
•	Support healthy risk-taking and expanded responsibilities
•	Foster leadership development and community engagement
•	Encourage deeper exploration of philosophical questions and beliefs
•	Prepare for upcoming life transitions with appropriate skills

Using This Algorithm
1.	Assess each dimension separately, considering observable behaviours and developmental appropriateness
2.	Calculate total score by adding all eight-themes/dimension scores
3.	Review individual dimension scores to identify specific strengths and growth areas
4.	Consider both chronological age and individual factors in interpretation (this is very important for holistic consideration)
5.	Implement targeted improvement strategies for dimensions scoring below age expectations (primary lookout)
6.	Reassess periodically (every 3-6 months) to track progress (depends on how soon we can start publishing the wellbeing. It may take few weeks to chat with the child and have enough data about their individual wellbeing to comment)
7.	Use results to inform supportive interventions and developmental opportunities (primary lookout)
